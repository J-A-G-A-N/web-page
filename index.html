Ethics in Social Media Abuse and Bias

Introduction

Definition of Social Media : Social media refers to online platforms and technologies that allow people to create, share, and interact with content and with each other. Eg : - Instagram, X, TikTok, Reddit , Forums.

Benefits of Social Media

  • Connectivity & Communication
  • Information & Awareness
  • Opportunities & Exposure
  • Learning & Collaboration
  • Entertainment

Core issues with social Media

  • Addictions
  • Privacy Violations
  • Cyberbullying & Harassment
  • Unrealistic Standards

Hate Speech and Harassment

  • Def: Content that incites violence, hatred, or discrimination against individuals or groups based on race, religion, gender, or other identities. EG : - Rohingya Genocide (Myanmar, 2017): Facebook was used to organize and incite violence against Rohingya Muslims. source

Doxxing

  • Def : Publishing or leaking someone’s private information (like home address, phone number) without consent, often to intimidate or threaten them.

Deepfakes and Non-Consensual Content

  • Def : Digitally manipulated media—often video or audio—used to falsely portray individuals, usually without consent. Real Life incedent :- Gal Gadot Deepfake (2017): Her likeness was used in explicit videos without consent. source

Exploitation of Children

  • Adults posing as teenagers groom minors on social media platforms, gaining trust before initiating harmful behavior.

Mental Health Impacts:

  • 41% of cyberbullying victims develop social anxiety and increased stress levels.
  • Teen victims of cyberbullying are 2.3 times more likely to develop symptoms of depression than their non-bullied peers.
  • Approximately 25% of cyberbullied kids reported engaging in self-harm, and 14% developed eating disorders

Effects on Perpetrators:

  • Students who engage in cyberbullying are approximately 3.17 times more likely to participate in delinquent behaviors.
  • Perpetrators of cyberbullying may develop increased aggression and antisocial behavior patterns, potentially leading to more severe criminal activities in adulthood.

Abuse from Corporate Companies to Users

  • Data Exploitation and Privacy Violations
  • Encouraging Addiction
  • Exploitative Labor of Influencers— especially minors — to promote brands constantly, often without contracts or proper mental health support.
  • Designing platforms and notifications to maximize screen time, knowing it can negatively impact mental health — especially in youth.

Solutions and Ethical Practices

  • Stronger regulations (e.g., EU Digital Services Act).
  • Algorithm audits for transparency.
  • Digital literacy education for users.
  • Bias testing in AI/ML systems.
  • Encouraging ethical design in tech development.

Navdeep’s additional Information

Recently, the Indian government faced significant pressure as the country experienced its largest protest concerning new farm laws. Farmers from various states across northern India united to voice their concerns, utilizing platforms like Twitter to spread their message.

Twitter, in particular, came under severe pressure from the Indian government to remove accounts and hashtags that were perceived to defame the government. In February 2021, Twitter temporarily withheld several accounts, including those of journalists, news agencies, and even celebrities, citing Indian laws on incitement and national security.

This action sparked widespread controversy regarding freedom of speech in the country. Critics argue that these large tech platforms yielded to significant political pressure from the government under the guise of national security.

The question arises: Should platforms prioritize compliance with local laws—even when this limits speech—or should they protect global standards of free expression? The farmers’ protest illustrates how moderation policies are not always applied consistently and can be influenced by the demands of powerful states.
